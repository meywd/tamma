## Executive Summary

Epic 1: Foundation & Core Infrastructure was planned as a 2-week foundation sprint with 13 stories aimed at establishing multi-provider AI abstraction, multi-platform Git integration, and hybrid orchestrator/worker architecture. **Key Finding:** Epic 1 is fundamentally incomplete - this retrospective represents a partial review of work-in-progress rather than a completed epic.

## What Didn't Go Well

### 1. Complete Implementation Stall

**Critical Issue:** 12 out of 13 stories never moved from "ready-for-dev" to implementation:

- No development work started on core interfaces
- No provider implementations begun
- No Git platform integration started
- CLI scaffolding not initiated

**Root Cause Analysis:**

- **Resource Allocation:** Development team not allocated to Epic 1
- **Priority Confusion:** Unclear if Epic 1 was active or planning phase
- **Dependency Management:** Stories marked as "ready" but not scheduled
- **Process Gap:** No mechanism to move stories from "ready" to "in-progress"

### 2. Timeline Misalignment

**Planning vs Reality:**

- Planned: 2-week sprint with 13 stories
- Reality: 1 research story completed over unknown period
- Gap: No active development sprints executed

**Impact:** Epic 1's foundation work is blocking Epic 2 (autonomous workflows) and all subsequent epics.

### 3. Missing Empirical Validation

**Research Limitation:** Story 1-0 identified but didn't complete empirical testing:

- Provider capabilities based on documentation, not actual testing
- Token usage estimates unverified
- Integration complexity not practically validated
- Cost projections based on assumptions

**Quote from Dana (QA Engineer):** "We're building our entire architecture on untested assumptions. That's risky."

---

## Key Insights & Lessons Learned

### 1. Research ‚â† Implementation

**Critical Insight:** Completing research stories doesn't automatically lead to implementation:

- Research stories can be completed independently
- Implementation requires separate resource allocation
- "Ready-for-dev" status needs active scheduling, not just preparation

### 2. Foundation Epics Need Different Management

**Process Learning:** Infrastructure epics behave differently from feature epics:

- Foundation work is less visible but more critical
- Dependencies cascade through entire project
- Requires explicit "implementation sprint" after research phase

### 3. Story Status Management Gap

**Process Issue:** Current sprint-status tracking doesn't distinguish between:

- Stories ready but not scheduled
- Stories actively being worked
- Stories blocked waiting for resources

### 4. Resource Planning Disconnect

**Organizational Learning:** Story preparation exceeded implementation capacity:

- Bob (SM) prepared 13 stories for development
- No developers allocated to implement them
- Created bottleneck at story-to-implementation transition

## Process Improvements

### 1. Story Lifecycle Management

**New Process Required:**

- Stories move from "drafted" ‚Üí "ready-for-dev" ‚Üí "scheduled" ‚Üí "in-progress" ‚Üí "review" ‚Üí "done"
- "Scheduled" status indicates resource allocation and timeline commitment
- Weekly story scheduling meeting to prevent bottlenecks

### 2. Foundation Epic Management

**New Guidelines:**

- Foundation epics require explicit "research phase" and "implementation phase"
- Research phase can complete independently
- Implementation phase requires separate resource planning
- Foundation work prioritized over feature development

### 3. Resource Planning Integration

**Required Change:**

- Story preparation capacity must match implementation capacity
- Resource allocation visible in sprint status
- Cross-team coordination between story preparation and implementation
